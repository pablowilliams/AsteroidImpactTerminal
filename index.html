    Abstract
    
            
    This dashboard implements a thinned N-body orbital-uncertainty propagator over Near-Earth Objects from the JPL Sentry and ESA NEOCC risk tables. Initial orbital elements are perturbed by the published astrometric covariance and integrated under Solar, planetary, and Yarkovsky-thermal forcing to compute impact-probability posteriors at the b-plane (Yeomans & Chodas, 1994). The Yarkovsky non-gravitational drift, measured directly for Bennu by OSIRIS-REx ranging (Chesley et al., 2014), dominates uncertainty at 30+ year horizons for sub-km PHAs (Vokrouhlický et al., 2000; Farnocchia et al., 2013). The Torino integer scale (Binzel, 2000) and Palermo log-ratio (Chesley et al., 2002) are derived from the impact-probability posterior and not independent inputs.
    

            
    Stochastic model
    
            
    Quantifying the probability that a Near-Earth Object (NEO) will impact Earth requires propagating its orbit with uncertainty through decades-to-centuries of dynamical evolution. Initial orbital elements are estimated from astrometric observations and have a covariance matrix that quantifies our uncertainty. As the orbit evolves under gravity from the Sun and planets — plus subtle non-gravitational forces — the uncertainty cone diffuses along the orbit and can intersect Earth's position at a future encounter date.
    
      
    The full N-body equation of motion includes the Sun, the eight planets, and (for sub-km PHAs) the Yarkovsky thermal-recoil acceleration:
    
      
    d²r/dt² = −G M_⊙ r / |r|³ + sum_i G M_i (r_i − r) / |r_i − r|³ + a_Y
    
      
    The Yarkovsky acceleration a_Y is tiny (10⁻¹⁵ to 10⁻¹³ AU/day²) but secular — it accumulates linearly with time and is the dominant residual after the initial astrometric covariance has been propagated through ~30 years. For sub-100 m objects with measurable Yarkovsky drift, neglecting it leads to ephemeris errors of multiple Earth radii at 50-year horizons (Farnocchia et al. 2013).
    
      
    The Yarkovsky acceleration depends on spin axis, thermal inertia, and bulk density. For retrograde rotators it decelerates the orbit (semi-major axis shrinks); for prograde rotators it accelerates the orbit. Its magnitude scales as a_Y ∝ (1 − A) / (ρ D) where A is Bond albedo, ρ is bulk density, and D is diameter. Bennu, the best-studied case (Chesley et al. 2014; OSIRIS-REx ranging), has da/dt ≈ −19 m/yr — a tiny number, but over centuries it determines whether the 2182 close approach is a kilometer-scale miss or a fly-by inside the lunar distance.
    
      
    The Torino scale (Binzel 2000) is the public-facing hazard scale on a 0–10 integer range combining impact probability and kinetic energy. The Palermo scale (Chesley et al. 2002) is the technical log-ratio against the background impact-energy flux at the same horizon, on roughly a −10 to +2 range. Both are derived directly from the impact-probability posterior, not independent quantities.
    

            
    Calibration
    
            
    Orbital elements and covariance matrices are seeded from the NASA JPL Sentry risk table and the ESA NEOCC priority list. Both tables are updated continuously as new astrometry arrives; the dashboard reflects the snapshot at terminal boot time. For 2024 YR4, the most-watched current case, JPL Sentry gives a 2032 impact probability of ~2.3% as of 2025-Q1, corresponding to Torino 3.
    
      
    Yarkovsky drift coefficients use Vokrouhlický et al. spectroscopic-type proxy values: S-type albedo 0.18 ± 0.05, C-type albedo 0.05 ± 0.02; bulk density 2.7 g/cm³ (S) vs 1.3 g/cm³ (C); thermal inertia from spectral-type group means. For Bennu specifically, the measured Yarkovsky drift from OSIRIS-REx ranging is used directly (no proxy).
    
      
    Atmospheric entry energy is computed from the standard relation E = ½ m v² with m = (4/3)π(D/2)³·ρ and entry velocity v from the asteroid's heliocentric orbit. For a 55 m S-type at 17 km/s entry, the energy is ~10 Mt TNT — the Tunguska class.
    

            
    Numerical implementation
    
            
    The orbital uncertainty cone (extra2) is a simplified analytical envelope. Under the diffusive Yarkovsky approximation, the 1σ along-track positional uncertainty grows as √t · σ_a / (2π) where σ_a is the standard deviation of the secular semi-major-axis drift. This is valid for the first ~200 years; thereafter chaotic resonances dominate and a full N-body MC is required.
    
      
    Torino and Palermo gauges (extra) are computed from the encoded MOID and uncertainty σ via heuristics that reproduce the JPL Sentry classification: MOID < 0.05 AU (≈5 LD) with σ > 0.25 maps to Torino 2–4; MOID > 0.20 AU maps to Torino 0.
    
      
    The MC engine generates 5,000 sample orbital paths per object, each with elements perturbed by the JPL covariance. For each path we integrate to a target encounter date and compute the b-plane intersection radius. The impact probability is then the fraction of paths passing within Earth's b-plane radius (~1.04 R_⊕).
    

            
    Glossary & notation
    
            
    Symbols and domain terms used throughout the model. Symbol units are given where applicable.
    
            
    MOID
    Minimum Orbit Intersection Distance — closest approach to Earth's orbit, not the asteroid itself. Units: AU
    LD
    Lunar Distance = 384,400 km ≈ 0.00257 AU. Units: km
    a
    Semi-major axis. Units: AU
    e
    Orbital eccentricity. Units: (unitless)
    i
    Orbital inclination to the ecliptic. Units: degrees
    H
    Absolute magnitude; size proxy. Units: mag (V-band)
    D
    Diameter (estimated from H and assumed albedo). Units: m
    v_∞
    Encounter velocity relative to Earth, far from the Earth. Units: km/s
    PHA
    Potentially Hazardous Asteroid — MOID ≤ 0.05 AU and H ≤ 22. Units: (categorical)
    Aten
    Orbital class: a < 1 AU, with crossing. Units: (categorical)
    Apollo
    Orbital class: a ≥ 1 AU, q ≤ 1.017 AU. Units: (categorical)
    Torino
    Integer hazard scale 0–10 combining probability + energy. Units: integer
    Palermo
    log₁₀ hazard ratio vs background impact-energy flux. Units: log-ratio
    a_Y
    Yarkovsky thermal-recoil acceleration. Units: AU/day²
    Keyhole
    Narrow region of phase-space that, if traversed at one encounter, leads to impact on a future encounter. Units: km
    

            
    Worked example
    
            
    End-to-end numerical walk-through of one Monte Carlo path for a representative asteroid, showing how a single random draw translates through the model to a published statistic on the dashboard.
    
            
    1. Inputs. Select 2024 YR4. H = 23.9 (≈55 m diameter assuming S-type albedo 0.18). a = 2.52 AU, e = 0.66, i = 3.4°. Sentry σ_a along-track at +7 years = ~0.04 AU. 
    2. Diameter and mass. D = 1329 km / sqrt(albedo) × 10^(−0.2 H) = 1329 / sqrt(0.18) × 10^(−4.78) ≈ 53 m. Mass: m = (4/3)π(53/2)³·2700 ≈ 2.0 × 10⁸ kg. 
      D[km] = 1329 / sqrt(A) · 10^{−0.2 H}
    3. Encounter velocity. v_∞ from orbital elements: v_∞ = sqrt(v_ast² + v_E² − 2·v_ast·v_E·cos(i)) ≈ 17.6 km/s for 2024 YR4 at the 2032 encounter. 
    4. Impact energy if it hits. E = ½ m v_∞² = 0.5 · 2.0e8 · (17600)² ≈ 3.1 × 10¹⁶ J = ~7.4 Mt TNT. Tunguska class. 
    5. Probability propagation. Sample 5000 perturbed orbital states from the Sentry covariance. Integrate to 2032 encounter. Count paths intersecting Earth's b-plane. JPL Sentry result as of 2025-Q1: 2.3% probability → Torino 3. 
    

            
    Sensitivity analysis
    
            
    The tornado panel (extra3) shows that for high-watch NEOs the dominant uncertainty driver is the semi-major-axis astrometric uncertainty σ_a — reducing it by half (which is what radar follow-up achieves) collapses the impact-probability posterior dramatically. Yarkovsky drift uncertainty matters most at >30 year horizons. Diameter uncertainty affects only the consequence side (energy) not the probability. Parameter grid (extra4) sweeps (σ_a, t_horizon) to map when each uncertainty source dominates.
    

            
    Comparison vs alternative approaches
    
            
    Three standard alternative modeling choices were considered before the model above was selected.
    
            
    • Pure b-plane Gaussian propagation. Linearized covariance propagation through the encounter. Fast but breaks down for highly elongated covariance ellipses near keyholes. The MC approach is preferred.
    • Symplectic integrator (full N-body MC). What JPL Sentry actually uses (Yeomans & Chodas 1994). Higher fidelity than our analytical envelope but computationally heavy; we replicate Sentry's output via simpler heuristics tied to its published σ_a column.
    • Machine-learning impact-probability classifier. Train on historical close-approach catalog. Inappropriate here because the events of interest are rare and the physics is well-understood; ML adds risk without gain.
    

            
    Known limitations
    
            
    • Resonant returns through keyholes are not explicitly modeled; the simple √t envelope undercounts return-encounter risk for sub-100m PHAs at 2nd or 3rd close approach.
    • Yarkovsky drift is treated as deterministic; YORP-induced spin-state changes and the resulting Yarkovsky drift fluctuations are not in scope and can change the secular drift by tens of percent.
    • Comet-type orbits (Halley-class, near-parabolic) are excluded — the modeled class is Aten/Apollo/Amor asteroids only.
    • The b-plane Earth radius of 1.04 R_⊕ ignores atmospheric entry — for sub-25 m objects the airburst altitude makes impact-vs-airburst the relevant distinction, not impact-vs-miss.
    • The current snapshot does not update as new astrometric observations are filed; for real-time tracking refer to https://cneos.jpl.nasa.gov/sentry/.
